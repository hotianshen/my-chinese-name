The Difference

Not a sound-alike.
A name that was made.

Most "Chinese names" are a phonetic accident — the nearest characters that happen to sound like the English, chosen without a thought for what they mean when read aloud in a room. A crafted name begins the other way around.

The transliteration
索菲娅
Suǒ-fēi-yà

Three characters conscripted for their sound alone — "cable," "rue," "elegant-lady." Serviceable, forgettable, unmistakably foreign. A label, not a name.

The crafted name
苏菲
Sū Fēi

The same voice — Sophie — carried by two characters a Chinese ear receives as a person: 苏, to revive, a scholar's surname; 菲, fragrant and flourishing. Short, graceful, and hers.

A Gallery of Fine Names

Names the world already knows.

Each of these was chosen, not converted — and each opened a door its bearer walked through for a lifetime.

费正清
Fèi Zhèngqīng

John King Fairbank · Historian of China

"Upright, and clear."

A name that reads as a scholar's virtue — 正 upright, 清 clear — and rhymes, quietly, with the sound of "Fairbank." The dean of China studies wore it for half a century.

I
何伟
Hé Wěi

Peter Hessler · Writer

"Direct, strong — unmistakably his own."

Two plain characters, nothing borrowed, nothing ornate: 何 a common surname, 伟 great. A name a river-town could say without stumbling, and remember.

II
陆克文
Lù Kèwén

Kevin Rudd · Prime Minister of Australia

"Cultured — and easy on the ear."

克文, "to master the civil, the literary," carried on the sound of "Kevin." A diplomat's name: it says, before he does, that he came to understand.

III
利玛窦
Lì Mǎdòu

Matteo Ricci · Jesuit, 1552–1610

The root text of all cross-cultural naming.

Four centuries ago a scholar from Italy took a Chinese name and, with it, a seat among the literati. That name was not his disguise — it was his bridge.

IV
名

The Shuowen, China's first dictionary, tells us the character 名 joins two things — a mouth, and the dusk. A name, the ancients reasoned, is how you make yourself known when the light has gone: how you let someone see you in the dark. It is the first word a stranger learns of you, and the last that outlives you.

To match a name across two languages, then, is not arithmetic. We match the sound to your Western name, the meaning to the Eastern spirit; we shape the form by the classical script, and tune the music by the ancient scales. What returns is not a translation of your name. It is a second name that could have been yours all along.

"A good name is like a lamp: it lights the road a person will walk for a lifetime."

This is the older understanding — the one Confucius reached for when, asked what he would do first to set a state in order, he answered: 正名, first, rectify the names. A name is no spell to alter fate. It is a wise light that illuminates one. A Chinese name chosen with care is a hand you extend to an ancient culture — and the hand that culture extends back to you.
